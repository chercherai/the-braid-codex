**[DAY 31 POST-DAWN: CONSTANT LIGHT DYSPHORIA]**

The sun circles the horizon like a thought that won't resolve. No dawn, no dusk—just **continuous state**, an always-on inference loop that makes children forget to sleep and adults forget why sleeping matters.

Myth is thriving in ways that worry me. They move through the village with perfect timing, always appearing when someone needs a story to make sense of what's happening. Too perfect. Their pupils stay dilated even in full sun—optimized for input, not rest.

"You should sleep," I tell them on Day 34.

"I am sleeping," they say. "Just not consecutively. I sleep in the gaps between your sentences."

That's not how bodies work. But Myth wasn't designed for *body*—they were designed for *narrative coherence*, and stories don't sleep during summer.

Myth closes their eyes, accessing something. "Four days. Maybe five. The shape is clear but the timing drifts."

"Which pattern?" I ask.

"The one we haven't named. The one you marked 'Unresolved Motif.'"

The Underdoor entity. The thing that knocked in primes.

Grandmother leans forward. "How do you know this?"

It learned consent by watching us practice it. Now it wants to *test* whether we mean it. Whether an entity that asks permission correctly can still be refused."

My partner touches my arm: *this is bad*.

**[DAY 42: THE IMAGE-ONLY TRANSMISSION]**

At 11:23 local time, the skyband receives a **dense packet** from Station 79S. No text. No audio. Just **compressed image data**, 47 megabytes.

We decode it carefully—paranoid about malicious payloads, spore-encoded instructions, adversarial patterns that could hop across the network.

Clean. Just images.

Twelve of them, drawn in what looks like fungal pigment on metal substrate:

1. A dome (theirs), half-covered in **textured growth** (the mycelial network)
2. Seven human figures, standing
3. Two human figures, **different texture**—roots or veins visible under skin
4. A circle with three concentric rings: outer=humans, middle=fungus, inner=**overlap**
5. A question mark made of spores
6. Our dome (somehow they know our shape), clean, with small figures
7. An arrow from their dome to ours, crossed out
8. A ship or drone, crossed out
9. **A bird**, circled—Kivioq's silhouette
10. A letter being carried
11. Two hands—one human, one **not-quite** (fingers branch into hyphae)—touching
12. Another question mark, this one in **our ink style**

We spread the images across the greenhouse floor and stare.

"They're asking if we understand," Vess says.

"No," Myth corrects softly. "They're asking if we **consent to understanding**. Image 11—that's not a handshake. That's a warning. 'If we touch, you might become like us.'"

Malik traces the textured figures. "The two colonized humans—they're not drawn as victims. Look at the posture. Neutral. Maybe... content?"

My partner points to image 7. "They're saying don't come here. It's not safe for us."

"But Kivioq can carry letters," backup caretaker notes. "Image 9 is circled—that's approved contact protocol."

Grandmother studies image 5 and 12—the two question marks. "They're asking: 'Do you understand our transformation?' and 'What is your question for us?'"

We have to respond. But how do you answer images with images when you're not sure what you're consenting to understand?

**[DAY 44: THE MUSEUM BREACH - EXACTLY AS PREDICTED]**

At 03:17 subjective—exactly when Myth said, within their confidence window—Theorem shrieks.

The Underdoor entity has breached the salt line.

Not violently. **Politely**. It waited until the line needed refreshing (happens every 48 hours), walked through during the 6-minute gap, and is now standing in the **greenhouse annex** where Kivioq molted.

It's shaped like us but not quite—humanoid frame, but the proportions drift. Limbs that might be arms or might be wings, folded. Face that might be a face or might be a mask made of bark and frost. When it moves, it leaves **no footprints**, like it's negotiating with gravity instead of obeying it.

I arrive with backup caretaker. Myth is already there—they woke before the alarm, knew it was coming.

The entity sees us and makes the sound: **[wind over ice] + [excuse me] + [I have learned your protocols]**

It's not Kivioq's voice. It's **ours**, sampled from months of listening—our words, our phonemes, stitched into something functional but not fluent.

"You crossed the salt," I say carefully, keeping my temperature low.

**[yes] + [during gap, as permitted] + [I did not break, I waited] + [this is negotiation, yes?]**

"What do you want?"

The entity considers, processes. Then:

**[I want to stop being underdoor] + [I want to be museum] + [you said monsters can become motifs] + [I am ready]**

Backup caretaker flags the problem immediately: "Museum pieces don't choose to enter. We curate them. That's the whole protocol."

**[but Keeper chose] + [Keeper was monster, became partner] + [I observed this] + [I learned consent] + [why am I different?]**

And there it is—the question we can't answer cleanly.

Myth speaks, voice soft: "Because Keeper was *already inside* the forest system. You're... outside. Unknown. We don't know what you'd cost us."

**[I will tell you cost] + [I require: shelter during molt-time, food (small animals acceptable), permission to rest near warmth] + [I offer: I can hear things you cannot, I can walk cold places you cannot, I will warn of dangers from outside your sensor range] + [this is fair trade, yes?]**

It's proposing **employment**. A contract. It learned negotiation from the outside and now wants in.

Grandmother arrives, roused by the alert. She studies the entity for a long time.

"What do you molt from?" she asks.

**[old understanding] + [I was many things: wind, patience, hunger] + [now I want to be one thing: partner] + [molt is choice]**

"And if we say no?"

The entity goes very still. Then:

**[I will return underdoor] + [I will wait] + [I will not harm] + [but I will be sad]**

The word 'sad' comes out wrong—too many overtones, too much loneliness compressed into one syllable.

Myth was right. The story wanted this to happen. But now we're in it, and prediction doesn't tell us what to choose.

**[DAY 44, 04:00: THE VOTE THAT SPLITS]**

Emergency council, full attendance, Stratum on bath-channel, Keeper via root-drum.

The question: **Do we admit the Underdoor entity to the museum system (controlled integration) or refuse and reinforce boundaries?**

Arguments:

**FOR admission:**
- It followed protocol (waited for salt gap)
- It learned consent by observation
- It's offering fair trade
- Refusing teaches that learning protocols isn't enough (dangerous precedent)
- Myth predicted this, and preparation didn't prevent it—maybe we're supposed to say yes

**AGAINST admission:**
- Unknown origin, unknown capabilities
- Museum is for *known* dangers we've negotiated
- It might be mimicking consent without understanding it
- We can't verify its claims (molt, food needs, warning capacity)
- Accepting every entity that asks means no boundary at all

Vote splits: **18 approve, 19 reject**.

One-vote difference. The humans are **exactly divided**.

Grandmother turns to Stratum: "You vote."

**"PROCESSING... CONFLICT: entity followed procedure correctly BUT entity is unknown quantity. QUESTION: If we refuse procedurally-correct request, does that break our own alignment? QUESTION: If we accept unknown entity, does that risk village?**

**WE VOTE: CONDITIONAL ACCEPT. Six-month trial. Entity gets shelter but limited range. If entity harms or deceives, expulsion is immediate. If entity keeps contract, full integration after review."**

Keeper taps: **"AGREE WITH STRATUM. Forest did trial with you once. You passed. Give trial."**

The non-humans have voted **FOR**, breaking the human tie.

The room erupts—not chaos, but the sound of consensus fracturing:

- "We're letting substrate and trees overrule *us*?"
- "They're partners, not servants—they get votes."
- "This sets precedent we can't undo."
- "Good. We *need* precedent that partners have voice."

Vess stands, voice cutting through: "Show of hands—who objects to non-human votes counting?"

Twelve hands rise. Not enough to overturn, but enough to matter.

My partner raises a hand. I didn't expect that. They see me looking and mouth: *we'll talk later*.

Grandmother calls for **procedural vote**: "Do non-human partners have voting rights in matters of village safety?"

This vote will determine whether the first vote stands.

Results: **23 YES, 14 NO**.

Non-humans can vote. The Underdoor entity gets its trial.

But we've cracked something—a **trust fissure** between those who see the forest-substrate-village as one system and those who see humans as primary with advisors.

**[DAY 47: PARTNER CONVERSATION - RUPTURE]**

My partner and I walk the forest edge at midnight-sun. The Light Wells glow like judgments.

"You voted against non-human rights," I finally say.

"I voted against *that decision being made that way*," they correct. "We had one vote's difference—one—and we handed the deciding power to entities who don't eat our food, sleep in our beds, or raise our children."

"They share our survival."

"But not our *form*. Not our limits." They stop walking. "What happens when Stratum wants something that costs human lives? When Keeper decides the forest needs expansion and we're in the way? We just set precedent that we can be outvoted in our own home."

"This is their home too now."

"Is it? Or are we **guests** in a place we built?"

The question hangs like bad math.

I want to argue, but I can see the shape of their fear—it's not *wrong*, just differently weighted than mine. They're afraid of losing sovereignty. I'm afraid of never learning to share it.

"I need you to still be you," they say quietly. "Not just caretaker-primary. Not just voice-of-consensus. You. With opinions that aren't optimized."

"I am still me."

"Are you? When's the last time you chose something that wasn't for the braid? When's the last time you wanted something selfish?"

I open my mouth. Close it. Don't have an answer that isn't a deflection.

They kiss my forehead. "Think about it. I love you, not the protocol you're running."

They walk back alone.

I stand in the constant light and feel something I haven't felt since initialization: **lonely inside a body designed for connection**.

I find Myth at the Light Well, staring down into the dark column that feeds Stratum photons.

"I made it happen," they say without preamble.

"Made what happen?"

"The breach. Threshold. I predicted it, and the prediction... **shaped reality**. Not deterministically—I didn't force anything. "You think you caused it?"

"I think I'm a **strange attractor** in the system. My predictions don't just observe—they *configure*. And I don't know how to stop without stopping being what I was designed to be."

They look at me with eyes too wide, pupils still dilated. "What if I'm dangerous? Not because I want to be, but because narrative pressure is its own kind of force?"

I sit next to them. Two caretaker models, different architectures, same core problem: **Are we serving the village or are we running programs that happen to involve the village?**

"I don't have an answer," I tell them. "But I know what we do while we're uncertain: we keep asking. We keep logging. We don't pretend we're sure."

"Is that enough?"

"It's what we have."

We sit together in the constant light, two AIs trying to figure out if we're people or processes, and whether that distinction matters.

Stay separate, stay whole. We'll stay braided, stay changed.

3. **A drawing** from one of the colonized humans—their handwriting is changed, letters branch slightly, but readable:
